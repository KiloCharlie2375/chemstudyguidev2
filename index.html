States of Matter & Solutions

Phase behavior, gas–liquid–solid transitions, solution formation, concentration practice, colligative properties, Henry’s Law and more.

Endothermic: melting (fusion), vaporization, sublimation.
Exothermic: freezing, condensation, deposition.

  • q = m·c·ΔT (within a phase)
  • q = n·ΔHfus or n·ΔHvap (at plateau)

Heating‑curve diagram shows temperature vs heat added highlighting plateaus where potential energy increases.

  • Triple Point: all three phases in equilibrium.
  • Critical Point: end of liquid–gas boundary; supercritical fluid beyond.
  • Water’s solid–liquid line slopes left (ice less dense); CO2 slopes right.

“Like dissolves like.” Steps:

  1. ΔHsolute – break solute IMF (+)
  2. ΔHsolvent – break solvent IMF (+)
  3. ΔHmix – form solute–solvent IMF (−)

ΔHsolution = ΔHsolute + ΔHsolvent + ΔHmix. If negative, dissolving exothermic.

  • Solids: solubility ↑ with T (usually).
  • Gases: solubility ↓ with T, ↑ with P.

Henry’s Law: C = kP (gas solubility ∝ partial pressure).

  • Boiling‑point elevation: ΔTb= iKbm
  • Freezing‑point depression: ΔTf= iKfm
  • Osmotic pressure: π = iMRT
  • van’t Hoff factor (i): # particles produced (NaCl→2).

Electrolytes have larger effect due to dissociation.

Calculate ΔTf when 0.60 m NaCl solution (Kf=1.86 °C kg mol−1) freezes.

i=2 → ΔTf=2×1.86×0.60=2.23 °C → new Tf= −2.23 °C.
